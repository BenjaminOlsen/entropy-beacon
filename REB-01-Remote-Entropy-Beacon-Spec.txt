PROJECT SPECIFICATION Project Name: Remote Entropy Beacon (REB-01)

  ------------------------------------------------
  0. Why Entropy Matters: The Invisible Foundation
  ------------------------------------------------

Right now, as you read this, your devices are burning through random
numbers at a staggering rate. Every time your phone opens a website,
it generates a secret random key and uses it to scramble the
connection. Every time you log in somewhere, the server mints a
random token to prove it's really you coming back. Every time a
software update lands on your laptop, a random-number-based
signature confirms it hasn't been tampered with.

Random numbers are the invisible scaffolding holding up digital
security. Not algorithms, not passwords — randomness. Here's why:

-   When you see that little padlock in your browser, the thing
    actually protecting your banking session is a random number that
    was generated a fraction of a second ago. If anyone could have
    guessed that number, the padlock is decoration.

-   The code your authenticator app shows you every 30 seconds? It
    was seeded from a random key. If the key was predictable at
    birth, every code it ever produces is predictable too.

-   Cryptocurrency wallets are, at their core, just very large
    random numbers. In 2013, a flaw in Android's random number
    generator let attackers drain Bitcoin wallets — not by cracking
    any encryption, but by guessing the randomness that created the
    keys in the first place.

-   Scientists running climate models, drug simulations, and
    financial forecasts all feed random numbers into their
    calculations. If the randomness is subtly biased, so are the
    results — and nobody notices.

This isn't theoretical. The history of randomness failures reads
like a thriller — except the victims are real:

-   In 2006, a Debian developer made a tiny, innocent-looking code
    cleanup to OpenSSL. He commented out two lines that looked like
    a bug. They weren't. Those lines were feeding entropy into the
    random number generator. For almost two years, every single SSL
    certificate, SSH key, and VPN secret generated on Debian and
    Ubuntu systems was drawn from a pool of roughly 32,000 possible
    values. That's not a typo. Thirty-two thousand. Servers that
    should have been protected by numbers too large to guess were
    instead protected by numbers a laptop could try in seconds. By
    the time anyone noticed, millions of keys were compromised.

-   In 2010, a team of hackers broke the PlayStation 3's entire
    security model — not through some exotic attack, but because
    Sony made a breathtaking mistake. The ECDSA signing algorithm
    requires a fresh random number for every signature. Sony used
    the same one. Every time. Once the hackers noticed that, they
    could work backwards from two signatures to extract Sony's
    master private key. With that key, anyone could sign code and
    the PS3 would trust it completely. Sony's billion-dollar content
    protection system was undone by a single static value where a
    random one should have been.

-   In 2012, researchers downloaded every publicly visible RSA key
    on the internet — millions of them — and simply checked whether
    any of them shared a prime factor. They did. A lot of them.
    Tens of thousands of devices — routers, firewalls, VPN
    appliances — had generated their keys at boot time, when the
    system had almost no entropy. Many devices booted identically:
    same hardware, same firmware, same startup sequence. So their
    "random" primes weren't random at all. When two keys share a
    prime, you can factor both of them in microseconds. Thousands
    of devices protecting real network traffic were quietly using
    keys that could be broken with a single math operation.

-   Perhaps the most chilling story involves Dual EC DRBG, a random
    number generator that NIST standardized in 2006 — with input
    from the NSA. Cryptographers quickly pointed out that the
    algorithm contained what looked like a backdoor: if you knew a
    secret relationship between two internal constants, you could
    predict all of its output. The warnings were largely ignored.
    RSA Security made it the default in their widely-used BSAFE
    toolkit. Juniper Networks used it in their VPN products. Then
    in 2015, someone discovered that Juniper's code had been
    quietly modified — the backdoor constants had been changed to
    different ones, meaning an unknown party had redirected the
    backdoor to themselves. Years of encrypted VPN traffic, from
    governments and corporations worldwide, may have been readable
    by whoever held the secret. The randomness wasn't broken by
    accident. It was broken on purpose, by design, inside a
    government standard.

The unsettling part is that bad randomness is completely silent.
There's no error message. No warning popup. A weak random number
looks exactly like a strong one. Your browser still shows the
padlock. The signature still verifies. Everything appears to work
perfectly — it just isn't actually protecting anything.

That's the problem the Remote Entropy Beacon is built to address.
Instead of trusting that randomness is "probably fine," this device
harvests it from real physical processes, tests it continuously, and
reports exactly how much genuine unpredictability it measured. No
assumptions. No black boxes. Just physics, math, and transparency.

  ---------------------------------------------------------------
  0.1 What Is Entropy, Why Does It Matter for Random Numbers,
      and How Can It Be Harvested?
  ---------------------------------------------------------------

The word "entropy" gets thrown around a lot in tech circles, often
loosely. So let's be precise about what it actually means — and
doesn't mean — in the context of generating random numbers.

What entropy is:

Entropy, in information theory, is a measure of uncertainty. Not
chaos, not disorder in the colloquial sense — uncertainty. Specifically,
it measures how surprised you should be by the next value in a
sequence. If you're flipping a fair coin, each flip carries one "bit"
of entropy: you genuinely cannot do better than a 50/50 guess. If the
coin is weighted so it lands heads 99% of the time, each flip carries
far less entropy — you can almost always guess right.

In cryptography, what we care about is min-entropy: the worst-case
measure. It doesn't ask "how uncertain are things on average?" It
asks "how predictable is the single most likely outcome?" That's a
much harder standard to meet, and it's the right one. An attacker
doesn't care about your average case. They care about their best
guess.

Here's a concrete example. Say you sample a sensor and it produces
values between 0 and 255. If every value is equally likely, you have
8 bits of entropy per sample — the maximum. But if the sensor tends
to hover around 120-130, with occasional excursions, the most likely
values might each appear 5-10% of the time. Your min-entropy per
sample might be only 3 or 4 bits. You're still getting a byte of
data, but there are only 3-4 bits of genuine unpredictability hiding
inside it. The rest is pattern.

Why entropy matters for random numbers:

Here's the thing that trips people up: a computer, left to itself,
is a deterministic machine. Give it the same inputs and it will
produce the same outputs, every time, forever. That's what makes
computers useful — and it's exactly what makes them terrible at
generating random numbers.

When your computer needs a random number, it typically uses what's
called a pseudorandom number generator (PRNG). This is an algorithm
that takes a small unpredictable value — called a seed — and
stretches it into a long stream of numbers that look random. The
important word there is "look." The output of a PRNG is entirely
determined by its seed. Same seed, same stream. Every time.

So the entire security of the system collapses down to one question:
where did the seed come from? If the seed has enough entropy — enough
genuine, physical unpredictability — then the PRNG output is
effectively unguessable. If the seed is weak, predictable, or
reused, the output inherits that weakness. No amount of algorithmic
cleverness downstream can create entropy that wasn't there at the
source. You can stir a cup of tea with the most elegant spoon in
the world, but if there's no sugar in the cup, the tea isn't getting
any sweeter.

This is why the Debian OpenSSL disaster was so devastating. The
algorithm was fine. The code was fine. But the entropy feeding the
seed was accidentally reduced to almost nothing, and everything
built on top of it — millions of keys that looked perfectly strong —
was quietly worthless.

How entropy can be harvested:

Since computers can't generate true randomness on their own, they
have to import it from the physical world. The question is: from
where?

Most computers already do this to some extent. Your operating system
quietly collects bits of unpredictability from keyboard timings,
mouse movements, disk seek times, and network packet arrivals. These
go into a system entropy pool (on Linux, this feeds /dev/urandom),
which seeds the PRNGs that applications rely on. For a laptop sitting
on someone's desk being actively used, this works reasonably well.

But it falls apart in exactly the environments where security matters
most: servers in data centers with no keyboard and no mouse. IoT
devices that boot identically every time. Embedded systems with no
user interaction at all. Virtual machines that are cloned from the
same snapshot. These are the systems generating your TLS certificates,
your VPN keys, your authentication tokens — and they're often the
most entropy-starved.

Dedicated entropy sources solve this by harvesting unpredictability
from physical processes that are fundamentally non-deterministic.
Common approaches include:

-   Thermal noise: Every electrical resistor produces tiny voltage
    fluctuations caused by the random motion of electrons. These
    fluctuations are quantum-mechanical in origin and genuinely
    unpredictable. Many hardware random number generators (like
    Intel's RDRAND instruction) use on-chip thermal noise sources.

-   Shot noise: When current flows through a semiconductor junction,
    individual electrons cross the barrier at random intervals. The
    statistical variation in their arrival times is another quantum
    effect that can be sampled for entropy.

-   Radioactive decay: The timing of individual atomic decay events
    is fundamentally random according to quantum mechanics. Some
    entropy sources use a small radioactive sample and a Geiger
    counter. It works beautifully, but try getting one through
    customs.

-   Atmospheric and environmental noise: Radio static, microphone
    hiss, photon arrival times, and vibration sensors all capture
    physical processes that are practically impossible to predict or
    reproduce. These sources are less "pure" than quantum effects —
    some of what they measure is deterministic (like a truck driving
    past a vibration sensor) — so they require more careful entropy
    estimation and conditioning.

-   Oscillator jitter: Two independent clock oscillators on a chip
    will never run at exactly the same frequency. The tiny drift
    between them is influenced by thermal noise and manufacturing
    variation. Sampling one clock with another captures this jitter
    as entropy. Many microcontrollers offer this as a built-in
    hardware RNG source.

The Remote Entropy Beacon takes the environmental noise approach: it
uses an accelerometer and a light sensor to capture physical
processes — vibration and light variation — that are happening in the
real world, right where the device is deployed. This is a deliberate
choice. The goal isn't to build the purest possible quantum entropy
source (those exist already, and they cost thousands). The goal is
to build something cheap, deployable, and transparent — a device
you can drop in a field, on a rooftop, or in a server room, and
have it continuously answer the question: "How much real randomness
is available here, right now, and can I prove it?"

That's what makes it a beacon. It doesn't just generate entropy. It
measures it, tests it, signs the results, and broadcasts them for
anyone to verify. The randomness isn't hidden inside a chip. It's
out in the open, with the math to back it up.

  ------------------------
  1. Project Description
  ------------------------

The Remote Entropy Beacon (REB-01) is a low-power, field-deployable
embedded system that:

-   Harvests entropy from natural physical processes (motion + light
    variation)
-   Continuously runs on-device entropy health tests
-   Estimates a conservative lower bound on min-entropy
-   Cryptographically hashes and signs entropy output
-   Transmits signed entropy reports over cellular (LTE-M / NB-IoT)
-   Uses a low-cost IoT SIM (1NCE) for long-term data connectivity

The device is designed to be: - Physically deployable in outdoor
environments - Cryptographically transparent - Statistically
self-reporting - Architecturally clean (MCU handles entropy, modem
handles networking)

2.1 Microcontroller (MCU)

Model: ST NUCLEO-L432KC
Chip:  STM32L432KC (Cortex-M4, ultra-low-power L4 series)
Board: Nucleo-32 form factor

Specifications:
-   256 KB Flash
-   64 KB SRAM
-   Cortex-M4 with FPU
-   Ultra-low-power sleep modes (same L4 family)
-   I2C, UART, SPI peripherals
-   Integrated ST-Link debugger
-   Nucleo-32 form factor (roughly half the size of Nucleo-64)

Reason: The entropy pipeline requires approximately 15-20 KB of RAM
(entropy sample windows, SHA-256 context, Ed25519 signing stack,
JSON report buffers, UART buffers, and MCV histogram). The L432KC
provides 64 KB SRAM — sufficient headroom without the excess of
larger boards. The Cortex-M4 core keeps Ed25519 performance
comfortable, and the L4 ultra-low-power family provides a path to
battery operation in future revisions. The smaller Nucleo-32 board
reduces physical footprint for field deployment.

No substitutes allowed.

  5. Functional Requirements (Prototype)
  ----------------------------------------

The device must:

1.  Sample accelerometer at 200 Hz bursts
2.  Sample light sensor at 50 Hz bursts
3.  Extract candidate entropy bits via:
    -   Delta sign method
    -   Timing jitter
4.  Run:
    -   Repetition Count Test (RCT)
    -   Adaptive Proportion Test (APT)
5.  Estimate min-entropy lower bound (MCV estimator)
6.  Hash candidate bits using SHA-256
7.  Produce 32 bytes entropy output per interval
8.  Sign report using Ed25519
9.  Transmit JSON report over cellular

  8. Security Considerations
  ----------------------------------

8.1 Report Integrity and Authenticity (Ed25519 Signing)

Every entropy report transmitted by the device is signed using
Ed25519. This ensures that:
-   Any recipient can verify the report was produced by this
    specific device
-   Any modification to the report payload (entropy data, metadata,
    timestamps) invalidates the signature
-   An attacker observing or intercepting network traffic cannot
    forge valid reports

Ed25519 was chosen over ECDSA because it uses deterministic nonce
generation — the signing process does not consume entropy. This
avoids the class of catastrophic failure demonstrated by the Sony
PS3 breach (see Section 0), where ECDSA nonce reuse exposed the
private key.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

8.2 Anti-Replay Protection (Monotonic Sequence Numbers)

Each signed report includes a strictly monotonic sequence number
in the signed payload. The sequence number:
-   Starts at 1 on first boot after provisioning
-   Increments by 1 for every report produced
-   Is stored in non-volatile memory (Flash) so it survives reboots
-   Is included inside the signed data, so it cannot be stripped
    or altered without breaking the signature

The receiving server must reject any report with a sequence number
equal to or lower than the last accepted value from that device.
This prevents an attacker from capturing a valid signed report and
replaying it later.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

8.3 Report Suppression Detection (Server-Side Gap Detection)

A man-in-the-middle cannot forge or alter signed reports, but they
can silently drop them. To detect suppression:
-   The server tracks the expected next sequence number per device
-   Any gap in the sequence triggers an alert
-   Reports include a UTC timestamp (sourced from the cellular
    modem's network time). The server cross-references timestamps
    against sequence numbers to detect time gaps consistent with
    suppression
-   Persistent gaps beyond a configurable threshold (e.g., 3
    consecutive missed intervals) raise an alarm

Note: suppression cannot be fully prevented at the device level —
a sufficiently positioned attacker can always block traffic. The
goal is detection, not prevention.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

8.4 Transport Encryption

Report confidentiality is a non-goal for the prototype. Entropy
reports are intended to be publicly verifiable, and the signed
payload contains no secrets. The entropy output itself is a
SHA-256 hash — it reveals nothing about the raw sensor data or
the device's internal state.

However, TLS should be used for the cellular transport when
practical, because:
-   It prevents traffic analysis (an observer cannot correlate
    report timing or size patterns)
-   It protects the device identity (the server endpoint and
    device public key would otherwise be visible)
-   The SIM7080G supports TLS natively via AT commands, so the
    implementation cost is low

For the prototype, TLS over the modem's built-in AT+CSSLCFG stack
is the recommended approach. Full mutual TLS (mTLS) is deferred
to post-prototype (see 8.6).

8.5 Key Provisioning [TBD]

The Ed25519 signing keypair must be established on the device and
the corresponding public key must reach verifiers through a trusted
channel. The provisioning strategy is not yet finalized.

Candidate strategies under consideration:

Strategy A — On-device key generation:
-   The device generates its own Ed25519 keypair using entropy
    harvested from its own sensors after an initial "entropy burn-in"
    period (e.g., 60 seconds of continuous sampling and health
    testing before the first key generation)
-   The public key is transmitted to the server over TLS during
    an initial enrollment phase
-   Advantage: The private key never exists outside the device
-   Risk: The device must have sufficient entropy at generation
    time — the burn-in period and health tests must be validated
    before this strategy is trusted

Strategy B — Offline injection during provisioning:
-   The keypair is generated on a secure provisioning workstation
-   The private key is written to the device's Flash over SWD/JTAG
    during initial programming
-   The public key is registered on the server at provisioning time
-   Advantage: Key quality is guaranteed by the provisioning
    workstation's entropy source
-   Risk: The private key exists momentarily on the workstation.
    The provisioning environment must be trusted and air-gapped

Strategy C — Hybrid:
-   The provisioning workstation generates a device identity
    certificate and a seed
-   The device combines the injected seed with locally harvested
    entropy to derive its signing key
-   Advantage: Key quality does not depend entirely on either source
-   Risk: More complex implementation; requires a key derivation
    scheme

Decision required before firmware development begins.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

8.6 Server Authentication [TBD]

The device signs its reports, but the prototype does not yet verify
the identity of the server it transmits to. A man-in-the-middle
could impersonate the server to:
-   Cause the device to waste bandwidth and power transmitting to
    a black hole
-   Observe transmission patterns (timing, frequency, payload size)
-   Respond with malicious AT-command payloads if the firmware
    parses server responses

Candidate strategies under consideration:

Strategy A — TLS with server certificate pinning:
-   The server's TLS certificate (or its public key hash) is
    embedded in the device firmware at provisioning time
-   The SIM7080G's TLS stack validates the server certificate
    against the pinned value before transmitting
-   Advantage: Simple, effective, supported by the modem's AT
    command set
-   Risk: Certificate rotation requires a firmware update or a
    pin rotation mechanism

Strategy B — Mutual TLS (mTLS):
-   Both the server and device present certificates during the
    TLS handshake
-   The device's client certificate is provisioned alongside its
    signing key (see 8.5)
-   Advantage: The server also verifies the device's identity at
    the transport layer, adding defense in depth beyond Ed25519
    report signing
-   Risk: Requires a certificate authority (CA) or a
    pre-shared trust model; increases provisioning complexity

Strategy C — Pre-shared symmetric key (PSK-TLS):
-   A shared secret is provisioned on both the device and server
-   TLS-PSK is used for mutual authentication without certificates
-   Advantage: Simpler than mTLS; no CA infrastructure needed
-   Risk: Symmetric key management; the shared secret must be
    provisioned securely (same challenges as 8.5)

Decision required before networking implementation begins.
